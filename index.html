A – C  ·  Classical Bits, Qubits & Superposition
A · Classical vs Quantum Computing
A classical computer works with bits — tiny switches that are either OFF (0) or ON (1). Every calculation is deterministic: the same input always gives the same output. A quantum computer uses qubits, which exploit quantum mechanics to exist in a combination of 0 and 1 simultaneously — until measured.
Property Classical Bit Qubit
Basic unit Bit (0 or 1) Qubit $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$
Possible states Exactly one: 0 or 1 Superposition $\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$
Parallelism Sequential Simultaneous amplitude over all states
Information 1 bit per operation Exponential state space ($2^n$ for $n$ qubits)
Measurement Non-destructive Collapses superposition — irreversible
Correlation Local (classical) Non-local entanglement (quantum)
Security basis Computational hardness Laws of physics (no-cloning theorem)
A classical computer with $n$ bits can be in exactly one of $2^n$ states. A quantum computer with $n$ qubits can represent all $2^n$ states simultaneously — exponential power from linear resources.
B · Qubit Definition
Think of a qubit like a coin spinning in the air — it's neither heads nor tails until it lands. Mathematically, a qubit is a unit vector in a 2-dimensional complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C}^2$.
General Qubit State
$$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$$
Here $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are complex numbers called probability amplitudes. The basis states $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ are the computational basis vectors:
$$|0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$$
Symbol Name Meaning
$|\psi\rangle$ Ket (state vector) The full quantum state of the qubit
$\alpha$ Amplitude for $|0\rangle$ Complex number; $|\alpha|^2$ = probability of measuring 0
$\beta$ Amplitude for $|1\rangle$ Complex number; $|\beta|^2$ = probability of measuring 1
$|0\rangle$ Computational basis state Analogous to classical bit = 0
$|1\rangle$ Computational basis state Analogous to classical bit = 1
Unlike a classical bit, a qubit carries both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ simultaneously. This is not just "unknown" — both values physically exist until measurement forces a collapse.
C · Superposition & Normalization
Superposition is the principle that a quantum system can be in multiple states at once. The constraint that total probability must equal 1 gives us the normalization condition:
$$|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$$
A perfect equal superposition — both outcomes equally likely — is:
$$|{+}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|0\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|1\rangle, \qquad \text{where } \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 + \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$$
State $\alpha$ $\beta$ $P(0)$ $P(1)$ Description
$|0\rangle$ $1$ $0$ $100\%$ $0\%$ Classical 0
$|1\rangle$ $0$ $1$ $0\%$ $100\%$ Classical 1
$|{+}\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ $50\%$ $50\%$ Equal superposition
$|{-}\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ $-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ $50\%$ $50\%$ Equal superposition (phase-flipped)
$|{+}\rangle$ and $|{-}\rangle$ have identical measurement probabilities in the computational basis, but they are physically different states — the phase $-1$ is detectable in other bases.
D – E  ·  Quantum Gates & Measurement
D · Quantum Gates — H, X, Z
Quantum gates are reversible operations on qubits, represented by unitary matrices ($U^{\dagger}U = I$). They are the quantum analogue of classical logic gates, but unlike classical gates they are always reversible.
Hadamard Gate (H)
The Hadamard gate creates equal superposition. It's the most important gate in quantum cryptography — it puts a qubit exactly "on the fence" between 0 and 1.
$$H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$$
$$H|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) = |{+}\rangle, \qquad H|1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) = |{-}\rangle$$
Applying H twice returns to the original state: $H^2 = I$. The Hadamard gate is its own inverse.
Pauli-X Gate (X) — Quantum NOT
The X gate flips a qubit from $|0\rangle$ to $|1\rangle$ and vice versa — the quantum equivalent of a classical NOT gate.
$$X = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad X|0\rangle = |1\rangle, \qquad X|1\rangle = |0\rangle$$
Pauli-Z Gate (Z) — Phase Flip
The Z gate leaves $|0\rangle$ unchanged but adds a phase of $-1$ to $|1\rangle$. Invisible in the computational basis, but critical for interference.
$$Z = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad Z|0\rangle = |0\rangle, \qquad Z|1\rangle = -|1\rangle$$
Gate Matrix Effect on $|0\rangle$ Effect on $|1\rangle$ Role
H $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle-|1\rangle)$ Create superposition
X $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ $|1\rangle$ $|0\rangle$ Bit flip (NOT)
Z $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$ $|0\rangle$ $-|1\rangle$ Phase flip
E · Measurement & Wavefunction Collapse
When we measure a qubit, the superposition collapses irreversibly to one of the basis states. The probability of each outcome is given by Born's rule:
$$P(0) = |\alpha|^2, \qquad P(1) = |\beta|^2$$
After measuring and obtaining outcome $|0\rangle$, the state instantly becomes $|0\rangle$ — all information about $\beta$ is destroyed. This is not a technical limitation; it is a fundamental law of quantum mechanics.
Expression Meaning
$P(0) = |\alpha|^2$ Probability of collapsing to $|0\rangle$
$P(1) = |\beta|^2$ Probability of collapsing to $|1\rangle$
$|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$ Probabilities must sum to 1 (normalization)
Post-measurement state Either $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$ — never both
⚠️ Measurement is the key to quantum security. An eavesdropper who measures a qubit cannot put it back in the original superposition — her interference leaves a detectable trace.
F – G  ·  Bloch Sphere & Entanglement
F · Bloch Sphere Interpretation
Every possible pure qubit state can be mapped to a point on the surface of a unit sphere — the Bloch sphere. The north pole is $|0\rangle$, the south pole is $|1\rangle$, and every other point is a superposition.
$$|\psi\rangle = \cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|0\rangle \;+\; e^{i\phi}\sin\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|1\rangle$$
Parameter Range Geometric meaning
$\theta$ $[0,\, \pi]$ Polar angle from north pole ($|0\rangle$)
$\phi$ $[0,\, 2\pi)$ Azimuthal angle — the relative phase
$\theta = 0$ North pole State $|0\rangle$ — certain to measure 0
$\theta = \pi$ South pole State $|1\rangle$ — certain to measure 1
$\theta = \pi/2,\, \phi = 0$ Equator (+x) State $|{+}\rangle$ — equal superposition
$\theta = \pi/2,\, \phi = \pi$ Equator (−x) State $|{-}\rangle$ — phase-flipped superposition
Gate Effects on Bloch Sphere
  • X gate → $180°$ rotation about the x-axis
  • Z gate → $180°$ rotation about the z-axis
  • H gate → $180°$ rotation about the diagonal (x+z)/√2 axis
Why the Sphere?
Any global phase factor $e^{i\gamma}$ is physically unobservable, so we only need two real parameters $\theta$ and $\phi$ to describe all physically distinct pure states — exactly the two coordinates of a sphere's surface.
The Bloch sphere is only valid for a single qubit. Entangled multi-qubit states cannot be described this way — they live in a much higher-dimensional space.
G · Quantum Entanglement
Entanglement is a quantum correlation with no classical analogue. When two qubits are entangled, measuring one instantly determines the state of the other — regardless of the distance between them. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance."
Separable vs Entangled States
A two-qubit state is separable if it can be written as a product of two independent single-qubit states:
$$|\psi_{\text{sep}}\rangle = |\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle = (\alpha_A|0\rangle + \beta_A|1\rangle)(\alpha_B|0\rangle + \beta_B|1\rangle)$$
An entangled state cannot be written this way. For example, the Bell state:
$$|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle) \neq |\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle \quad \text{for any } |\psi_A\rangle, |\psi_B\rangle$$
Property Separable State Entangled State
Factorizability $|\psi_A\rangle \otimes |\psi_B\rangle$ Cannot be factored
Measurement correlation Independent outcomes Perfectly (anti-)correlated outcomes
After measuring qubit A Qubit B unchanged Qubit B instantly determined
Example $|0\rangle \otimes |{+}\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$
Entanglement is the resource that makes quantum teleportation and the E91 cryptographic protocol possible. It is the engine of quantum information science.
H  ·  Bell States
The four Bell states (also called EPR pairs) are the maximally entangled two-qubit states. They form an orthonormal basis for the two-qubit Hilbert space and are the fundamental building block of quantum cryptography and teleportation.
The Four Bell States
$|\Phi^+\rangle$ — Phi Plus
$$|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$$
Measuring Alice as 0 → Bob is 0. Measuring Alice as 1 → Bob is 1. Perfect correlation.
$|\Phi^-\rangle$ — Phi Minus
$$|\Phi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |11\rangle)$$
Same correlations as $|\Phi^+\rangle$ in the computational basis, but opposite relative phase.
$|\Psi^+\rangle$ — Psi Plus
$$|\Psi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle + |10\rangle)$$
Alice 0 → Bob 1. Alice 1 → Bob 0. Perfect anti-correlation.
$|\Psi^-\rangle$ — Psi Minus
$$|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle - |10\rangle)$$
Anti-correlated with phase flip. This state is rotationally invariant — special in entanglement theory.
Quantum Circuits — Complete Bell State Basis
Each Bell state is produced by a distinct two-qubit circuit. All start from $|00\rangle$. The H gate creates superposition on $q_0$, the CNOT entangles both qubits, and optional Z / X gates on $q_1$ select the phase and correlation.
$|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$
$|\Phi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |11\rangle)$
$|\Psi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle + |10\rangle)$
$|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle - |10\rangle)$
Gate legend: Hadamard CNOT (●⊕) Phase flip Bit flip (NOT) Measure
Creating Bell States from $|00\rangle$
All four Bell states can be created by applying a Hadamard gate to the first qubit, then a CNOT gate targeting the second qubit:
$$|00\rangle \xrightarrow{H \otimes I} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)|0\rangle \xrightarrow{\text{CNOT}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle) = |\Phi^+\rangle$$
Bell State Formula Correlation Phase Used in
$|\Phi^+\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$ Correlated $+$ E91, Teleportation
$|\Phi^-\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |11\rangle)$ Correlated $-$ Dense coding
$|\Psi^+\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle + |10\rangle)$ Anti-correlated $+$ Superdense coding
$|\Psi^-\rangle$ $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle - |10\rangle)$ Anti-correlated $-$ E91 (singlet state)
In this simulator, the E91 experiment distributes $|\Phi^+\rangle$ pairs. Alice and Bob each receive one photon from each pair, measuring in random bases. Their correlated results become the shared secret key.
I  ·  BB84 Quantum Key Distribution
BB84 (Bennett & Brassard, 1984) was the world's first quantum cryptographic protocol. It allows Alice and Bob to generate a shared secret key over an insecure channel, with security guaranteed by the laws of quantum physics — not computational difficulty.
The Four BB84 States
Alice encodes each key bit using one of four quantum states drawn from two conjugate bases:
Bit Basis State Polarisation
$0$ Rectilinear $(+)$ $|0\rangle$ Horizontal ↔
$1$ Rectilinear $(+)$ $|1\rangle$ Vertical ↕
$0$ Diagonal $(\times)$ $|{+}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)$ Diagonal ↗
$1$ Diagonal $(\times)$ $|{-}\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle-|1\rangle)$ Anti-diagonal ↘
Four-Step Protocol
Step 1 — Preparation & Transmission
Alice picks a random bit and a random basis for each photon. She prepares and sends the photon in the corresponding state. Eve, if present, cannot copy it — the no-cloning theorem forbids perfect quantum copying.
Step 2 — Bob's Measurement
Bob independently picks a random basis for each photon. If he picks the same basis as Alice, he always gets the correct bit. If he picks a different basis, he gets a random result — useless for the key.
Step 3 — Basis Sifting
Alice and Bob publicly announce their basis choices (not the bits!). They keep only the bits where their bases matched. On average, this is $\approx 50\%$ of all transmitted bits.
$$\text{Key rate} \approx \frac{N}{2}$$
Step 4 — Error Checking
They reveal a small sample of their sifted key bits and compute the Quantum Bit Error Rate (QBER). If QBER exceeds the security threshold, they abort and try again.
QBER — Security Threshold
$$\text{QBER} = \frac{\text{Number of errors}}{\text{Total sifted bits}} \times 100\%$$
$$\text{Secure if:} \quad \text{QBER} < 11\%$$
QBER Range Interpretation Action
$0\% - 5\%$ Clean channel — no eavesdropping detected Secure
$5\% - 11\%$ Possible noise or weak eavesdropping Caution
$> 11\%$ Eve almost certainly present — key compromised Abort
Why Eve Gets Caught
When Eve intercepts a photon, she must measure it — but she doesn't know Alice's basis. Half the time she picks the wrong basis, forcing the photon into a wrong state. When she re-sends it to Bob, she introduces errors:
$$P(\text{error from Eve}) = \frac{1}{4} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{QBER}_{\text{Eve}} = 25\%$$
Even a "perfect" eavesdropper following the laws of quantum mechanics will introduce a QBER of 25% — well above the 11% threshold. She cannot hide.
J  ·  E91 Protocol & Bell Inequality
The E91 protocol (Ekert, 1991) takes a fundamentally different approach from BB84. Instead of Alice sending prepared photons to Bob, a central source distributes entangled photon pairs — Alice and Bob each receive one photon from each pair. Security is certified not by error rates, but by a direct test of quantum mechanics: the Bell inequality.
Protocol Overview
Step 1 — Entanglement Distribution
A source emits pairs of photons in the Bell state $|\Phi^+\rangle$. One photon flies to Alice, its entangled partner to Bob. Neither photon has a definite spin until measured.
$$|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$$
Step 2 — Independent Measurement
Alice and Bob each independently choose a random measurement direction (basis) for their photon. They never communicate their choices before measuring. The entanglement ensures their results are correlated even across arbitrary distances.
Step 3 — Basis Sifting
They publicly compare which basis they used for each pair. Pairs where they chose matching bases give perfectly correlated bits — these become the secret key. Mismatched-basis results are used for the Bell test.
Step 4 — Bell Test (Security Proof)
Using the mismatched-basis measurements, they compute the CHSH parameter $|S|$. A value above 2 confirms genuine quantum entanglement — and proves no eavesdropper could have pre-determined the key bits.
The CHSH Bell Inequality
The Clauser–Horne–Shimony–Holt (CHSH) inequality tests whether nature is governed by local hidden variables (classical physics) or quantum mechanics. Alice measures with settings $a$ or $a'$, Bob with $b$ or $b'$. Each measurement result is $\pm 1$.
Correlation Function
$$E(a,b) = \langle \hat{A}_a \otimes \hat{B}_b \rangle = P(+,+\,|\,a,b) + P(-,-\,|\,a,b) - P(+,-\,|\,a,b) - P(-,+\,|\,a,b)$$
CHSH Parameter
$$S = E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')$$
The Inequality
$$|S| \leq 2 \qquad \text{(classical / local hidden variable limit)}$$
$$|S| \leq 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828 \qquad \text{(quantum maximum — Tsirelson's bound)}$$
$|S|$ Value Physical Interpretation Security Status
$|S| \leq 2$ Consistent with classical (local) physics — no entanglement proven Not quantum-secure
$2 < |S| \leq 2\sqrt{2}$ Violates Bell inequality — genuine quantum entanglement confirmed Quantum-secure
$|S| = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$ Tsirelson's bound — maximum quantum violation (ideal conditions) Perfect entanglement
$|S| > 2\sqrt{2}$ Impossible — violates quantum mechanics itself Error / noise
Optimal Measurement Angles
The maximum violation $|S| = 2\sqrt{2}$ is achieved at these measurement angles:
$$a = 0°, \quad a' = 90°, \quad b = 45°, \quad b' = 135°$$
At these angles, the correlation between Alice and Bob's results is:
$$E(a,b) = -\cos(b - a) = -\cos(45°) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$
Why Bell Test = Security Proof
If an eavesdropper (Eve) had pre-determined the measurement outcomes using classical hidden variables, she would be constrained by $|S| \leq 2$. The fact that Alice and Bob observe $|S| > 2$ proves:
  • The photons were genuinely entangled — not pre-programmed classical particles
  • No hidden variable (including Eve) could have predetermined the results
  • Eve cannot have a copy of the key bits — she would have disturbed the entanglement and reduced $|S|$ below 2
In this simulator, watch the $|S|$ value in the top-right corner during the E91 experiment. With Eve OFF and no noise, you will see $|S| \approx 2.66$–$2.82$. Turn Eve ON and observe $|S|$ drop — the Bell inequality is violated less strongly, and the key is compromised.

2 $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$ Normalization Total probability must equal 1
3 $H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}$ Hadamard matrix Creates equal superposition
4 $H|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)$ Hadamard on $|0\rangle$ Maps $|0\rangle$ to $|{+}\rangle$
5 $P(0) = |\alpha|^2,\; P(1) = |\beta|^2$ Born's rule Measurement outcome probabilities
6 $|\psi\rangle = \cos\!\tfrac{\theta}{2}|0\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin\!\tfrac{\theta}{2}|1\rangle$ Bloch parameterization All pure qubit states on unit sphere
7 $|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)$ Bell state Φ⁺ Maximally entangled correlated pair
8 $|\Phi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |11\rangle)$ Bell state Φ⁻ Correlated with phase flip
9 $|\Psi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle + |10\rangle)$ Bell state Ψ⁺ Maximally entangled anti-correlated
10 $|\Psi^-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle - |10\rangle)$ Bell state Ψ⁻ Anti-correlated singlet state
11 $\text{QBER} = \frac{\text{errors}}{\text{sifted bits}} \times 100\%$ QBER BB84 eavesdropping metric; secure if $< 11\%$
12 $S = E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')$ CHSH parameter Bell inequality test value
13 $|S| \leq 2$ Classical bound Any local hidden variable theory
14 $|S| \leq 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$ Tsirelson's bound Maximum quantum violation
15 $|S| > 2$ Quantum security criterion Confirms entanglement — E91 key is secure
Glossary
Term Definition
Qubit Quantum bit — unit vector in $\mathbb{C}^2$; can be in superposition of $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$
Superposition A linear combination of basis states; collapses to one state upon measurement
Entanglement Non-separable correlation between two or more qubits; measurement of one instantly determines the other
Basis A set of orthonormal states used for measurement (e.g. computational $\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\}$ or diagonal $\{|{+}\rangle, |{-}\rangle\}$)
Unitary gate A reversible quantum operation; $U^\dagger U = I$; preserves normalization
No-cloning theorem It is impossible to perfectly copy an unknown quantum state — fundamental to quantum security
QBER Quantum Bit Error Rate — fraction of sifted key bits that are wrong; BB84 security metric
Bell inequality A bound on correlations in any classical (local hidden variable) theory; violated by quantum entanglement
CHSH parameter $S$ Specific form of Bell inequality; classically $|S|\leq 2$, quantum maximum $|S|=2\sqrt{2}$
Tsirelson's bound The maximum possible violation of the CHSH inequality by any quantum state: $2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$
Sifting The step where Alice and Bob discard bits where they used different measurement bases
One-time pad Encryption where each bit of plaintext is XORed with one bit of a random key of the same length; perfectly secure when key is truly random

System Insights: Real BB84 requires true single-photon sources, but attenuated lasers are widely used in practical QKD systems (Weak Coherent Pulse / Decoy-state BB84) to prevent photon-number splitting (PNS) attacks.

Limitation: Multi-photon pulses can introduce security vulnerabilities, mitigated using decoy-state techniques.

Note:
• Cost varies significantly based on detector sensitivity and optical precision.
• The single-photon detector (APD/SPAD) dominates the total system cost.
